Part I — Babbling Brook

The original SuperCollider program produces a bubbling, brook-like sound using a resonant high-pass filter whose cutoff frequency is continuously modulated by a second, slowly-varying noise signal: 

{RHPF.ar(LPF.ar(BrownNoise.ar(), 400), LPF.ar(BrownNoise.ar(), 14) * 400 + 500, 0.03, 0.1)}.play

Reading from the inside out: the audio source is brown noise passed through a 400 Hz low-pass filter, which removes the brighter high-frequency content and gives a warmer, denser texture. A second independent brown noise signal is low-pass filtered at 14 Hz — far too slow to be heard as pitched audio, so it becomes a slowly wandering control signal. Scaled by 400 and offset by 500, it produces a frequency that drifts roughly between 100 and 900 Hz. This drives the cutoff of the resonant high-pass filter (RHPF), which sweeps through the audio signal and creates the gurgling character.

Signal flow — Part I

AUDIO PATH BrownNoise #1 → LPF 400 Hz → RHPF → GainNode 0.1 → destination

MODULATION PATH (→ RHPF frequency param) BrownNoise #2 → LPF 14 Hz → GainNode ×400 → rhpf.frequency
ConstantSource 500 → rhpf.frequency

The main challenge in translating this to Web Audio was implementing the arithmetic * 400 + 500 on an audio signal. Web Audio's AudioParam interface accepts node connections and sums all connected signals automatically. So instead of computing the expression directly, I connected a GainNode (handling the ×400 scaling) and a ConstantSourceNode (providing the +500 DC offset) both to rhpf.frequency. Web Audio adds them together, which is exactly what the SuperCollider expression does.

Part II — Rain / Water Drops

For Part II I chose to synthesize rain, drawing from Farnell's physical description of water drops impacting a surface. The core observation is that rain has two distinct perceptual layers: individual drop impacts, which are short transient events with a tonal character, and a continuous background wash that emerges as drop density increases.

I modeled each drop as a white noise burst passed through a tight bandpass filter, shaped with an instant-attack / exponential-decay gain envelope. The bandpass filter gives each drop a pitched quality (the resonance of the water surface), while the decay envelope replicates the physical damping. Per-drop randomization in frequency (±30%), gain (±40%), and decay time (±50%) ensures no two drops sound identical.

Drops are scheduled using a Poisson process — inter-arrival times are drawn from an exponential distribution with mean 1/rate seconds. This avoids the mechanical regularity of a fixed timer and better matches the statistical character of natural rainfall.

The background layer is simply white noise run through a low-pass filter at around 2 kHz, kept at a low gain. At high drop rates the two layers blend convincingly into continuous rain; at low rates the individual drops are clearly audible.

Signal flow — Part II

PER-DROP PATH (triggered ~30×/sec by Poisson scheduler) WhiteNoise burst → BPF ~2 kHz, Q=8 → GainNode (envelope) → destination

BACKGROUND WASH PATH WhiteNoise (looped) → LPF 2 kHz → GainNode 0.08 → destination

Synthesis types and design choices

Both parts use subtractive synthesis — starting from spectrally rich noise sources and carving out the desired character with filters. For Part I, the brown noise source was already specified; its lower spectral density compared to white noise gives a warmer, less harsh starting texture. For Part II, white noise was more appropriate because individual water drops contain a broad, bright transient.

Part I additionally uses frequency modulation of a filter (sometimes called filter FM or LFO-driven filtering), where a second noise signal at sub-audio rate becomes a control voltage for the RHPF cutoff. This creates the characteristic gurgling motion without any explicit timing or rhythm — it emerges from the randomness of the noise itself.

Part II uses a form of granular synthesis in spirit, though implemented simply: each drop is a short grain with its own envelope and filter settings, triggered stochastically. The Poisson scheduling is a deliberate choice over a fixed-rate timer because real rainfall has no underlying pulse — grains arrive randomly and occasionally cluster.

One thing I found interesting: if you increase the drop rate high enough in Part II, the individual events become indistinguishable and the sound approaches the continuous noise wash of the background layer. The two layers converge. That emergent behavior wasn't planned — it just falls out of the physical model naturally.
